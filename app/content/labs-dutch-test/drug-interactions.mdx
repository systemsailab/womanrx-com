<TLDR>
The DUTCH test measures sex hormone and adrenal metabolites in dried urine. Dozens of drugs, topical hormones, adaptogens, and even licorice root can push results high or low. Knowing what you are taking, and telling your clinician before the test, is the single most important step toward a result you can actually use.
</TLDR>

## What the DUTCH Test Actually Measures

The DUTCH (Dried Urine Test for Comprehensive Hormones) is a functional hormone panel developed by Precision Analytical that goes beyond a single serum estradiol draw. It quantifies urinary metabolites of estrogen, progesterone, androgens, and adrenal hormones using liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS), which is the same analytical backbone used in many hospital endocrine labs [1](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31550598/).

What makes it clinically distinctive is the metabolite mapping. Rather than reporting only estradiol (E2), it shows how your body processes estrogen downstream: how much becomes 2-hydroxyestrone (protective pathway) versus 4-hydroxyestrone or 16-alpha-hydroxyestrone (pathways linked in some observational data to higher breast cancer risk) [2](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26243470/). This metabolite ratio, sometimes called the 2:16 ratio, has been a subject of ongoing research, though its clinical predictive value remains debated among endocrinologists. Be clear: the 2:16 ratio is a biomarker under investigation, not a validated screening tool for breast cancer risk per ACOG or the Endocrine Society [3](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/committee-opinion/articles/2017/12/hormone-therapy-in-primary-ovarian-insufficiency).

The panel also reports:

- **Free cortisol and free cortisone** collected across four time points to approximate a diurnal cortisol curve
- **DHEA-S** as an adrenal androgen marker
- **Testosterone and its metabolites** (5a-androstanediol glucuronide, etiocholanolone)
- **Melatonin** (MT6s), useful when sleep disruption and circadian rhythm are part of the clinical picture
- **Organic acid markers** including markers of B12 status, oxidative stress, and neuroinflammation (DUTCH Plus version)

### Why Urine Metabolites Rather Than Serum

Serum measures circulating hormone at a single snapshot. Urine metabolites reflect what your liver and gut have done with those hormones over hours. Both have a place. For monitoring topical hormone replacement therapy, however, serum consistently underestimates absorption, whereas urinary metabolites may more accurately reflect tissue exposure [4](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30803772/). That distinction matters enormously when you are trying to figure out whether your dose of vaginal DHEA or transdermal estradiol is actually reaching target tissues.

## Normal DUTCH Test Ranges by Life Stage

"Normal" on the DUTCH test is not a single number. It shifts with age, menstrual cycle phase, reproductive status, and body composition. The reference ranges Precision Analytical uses are derived from their own normative database and are not standardized across laboratories the way TSH or HbA1c are. Keep that in mind.

### Reproductive Years (Cycling Women)

The DUTCH test is typically collected on cycle days 19 to 22 (luteal phase) to capture progesterone metabolites at their peak [5](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31550598/). Collecting at the wrong phase is itself a form of result distortion. In the luteal phase, a clinically reasonable total progesterone metabolite (pregnanediol + allopregnanolone) generally falls above 1,000 mcg/g creatinine, though this varies by individual cycle length and luteal adequacy.

Estrogen metabolites in reproductive-age women vary considerably. A rough guide:

| Metabolite | Typical luteal-phase range |
|---|---|
| Estradiol (E2) | 3.0 to 9.0 mcg/g Cr |
| Estrone (E1) | 4.0 to 12.0 mcg/g Cr |
| Estriol (E3) | 1.0 to 7.0 mcg/g Cr |
| 2-OH Estrone | 2.0 to 10.0 mcg/g Cr |
| 4-OH Estrone | 0.2 to 1.0 mcg/g Cr |
| 16-OH Estrone | 1.0 to 5.0 mcg/g Cr |

These are approximate guidance ranges. Your clinician should interpret your result against the Precision Analytical lab report reference intervals, which are age-stratified [1](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31550598/).

### Perimenopause

Perimenopause is the phase where DUTCH results are most volatile and most easily misread. Ovarian function fluctuates cycle to cycle. A single DUTCH test collected during an anovulatory cycle will show flat progesterone metabolites even if your next cycle is fully ovulatory. The Menopause Society (formerly NAMS) does not endorse any single hormone test, including the DUTCH, as diagnostic for perimenopause transition because no single result captures the variability [6](https://menopause.org/professional-resources/for-clinicians).

Cortisol patterns are also frequently disrupted in perimenopause. Falling estrogen reduces the buffering effect estrogen has on the HPA axis, and many perimenopausal women show a flattened diurnal cortisol curve on the DUTCH even without a primary adrenal disorder [7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28661841/).

### Postmenopause (Not on HRT)

Without ovarian estrogen production, estrogen metabolites fall substantially. Postmenopausal reference ranges on the DUTCH are lower across the board. Adrenal DHEA becomes the dominant androgen source, and DHEA-S production also declines roughly 10% per decade after age 30 [8](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16210377/).

### Postmenopause (On HRT)

This is where drug interference becomes the most clinically relevant issue. See the full section below.

## Drugs and Supplements That Distort DUTCH Test Results

This is the core clinical question. The DUTCH test is a metabolite panel, so anything that alters hormone production, metabolism, secretion, or renal excretion has the potential to change what the test reads. The changes are not subtle. Some drugs shift results by two to ten times normal reference ranges.

### Drugs and Supplements That RAISE DUTCH Test Values

**Exogenous hormones are the most common confounders.**

**Topical (transdermal/vaginal) estradiol.** Serum estradiol levels during topical therapy can look modest, but urinary estrogen metabolites, especially E2, E1, and 16-OH estrone, are dramatically elevated because the DUTCH captures the full metabolite load. A woman using 0.05 mg/day of transdermal estradiol patch may show DUTCH estrogen metabolites three to five times above postmenopausal reference ranges [4](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30803772/). This is not dangerous; it is a pharmacological effect. But it means a DUTCH result cannot be interpreted as "endogenous" hormone status while on transdermal therapy.

**Oral estradiol.** Oral estradiol undergoes first-pass hepatic metabolism, substantially increasing E1 relative to E2 and raising 16-OH estrone. This produces an estrogen metabolite profile that looks different from transdermal delivery, with proportionally more E1 and 16-OH estrone [9](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19818635/).

**Topical progesterone (bioidentical creams).** Over-the-counter progesterone creams create a striking discordance: serum progesterone may remain low (because progesterone is sequestered in fat and red blood cells rather than circulating freely), while urinary progesterone metabolites, particularly pregnanediol, are markedly elevated on the DUTCH [10](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10095367/). This is one of the most important misinterpretations in functional medicine practice. A woman told her progesterone is "fine" based on a DUTCH result while using a progesterone cream may have supraphysiologic tissue exposure that serum testing would miss entirely, or the opposite, a falsely reassuring urinary picture despite poor systemic delivery. Neither assay alone settles the question.

**Oral micronized progesterone (Prometrium).** Oral progesterone dramatically increases allopregnanolone (a neuroactive progesterone metabolite) on the DUTCH. This is pharmacologically real and clinically relevant for sleep and anxiety, but it means your DUTCH allopregnanolone reading while on 100 mg or 200 mg nightly oral progesterone reflects drug effect, not endogenous production [11](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21234783/).

**DHEA supplements (oral or topical).** Even low-dose DHEA (10 to 25 mg) substantially raises DHEA-S, testosterone metabolites, and estrogen metabolites on the DUTCH, particularly in postmenopausal women whose baseline production is low [12](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11239500/). Intrarosa (vaginal DHEA, prasterone 6.5 mg) raises local vaginal androgens significantly, with detectable but lower systemic metabolite elevation.

**Hydrocortisone and prednisone.** Exogenous glucocorticoids raise total free cortisol and cortisone on the DUTCH dose-dependently. Even inhaled corticosteroids at higher doses (fluticasone 500 mcg/day or above) can suppress endogenous cortisol and distort the diurnal pattern [13](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15356419/).

**Adaptogenic supplements.** Ashwagandha at 300 to 600 mg/day has been shown in randomized controlled trial data to reduce cortisol by up to 27.9% compared with placebo [14](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23439798/). This is a suppressive effect on cortisol that may make adrenal output look lower than baseline on the DUTCH. Licorice root (glycyrrhizin) blocks 11-beta-HSD2, the enzyme that converts active cortisol to inactive cortisone, causing free cortisol to rise and cortisone to fall on the DUTCH even with no change in HPA axis function [15](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11794746/).

**Biotin (vitamin B7) at high doses.** Biotin above 10 mg/day interferes with some immunoassay-based hormone tests, though the DUTCH uses LC-MS/MS rather than immunoassay. The FDA issued a safety communication about biotin interference in 2019 [16](https://www.fda.gov/medical-devices/safety-communications/fda-warns-biotin-may-interfere-lab-tests-fda-safety-communication). The DUTCH is less susceptible than serum immunoassays, but stopping high-dose biotin two to three days before testing remains a reasonable precaution.

### Drugs and Supplements That LOWER DUTCH Test Values

**Combined oral contraceptives (COCs).** This is the most clinically significant suppressor. COCs suppress LH and FSH via the hypothalamic-pituitary axis, shutting down ovarian estradiol and progesterone production almost completely. DHEA-S also falls by roughly 20 to 30% [17](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24128671/). A DUTCH test drawn while a woman is on COCs measures the drug and its metabolites, not her own hormone milieu. The test is not clinically useful for assessing endogenous hormone balance on COCs, and Precision Analytical's own sample reports flag this explicitly. COC users evaluating for PCOS-related androgen excess should wait at least three months after stopping before running a DUTCH.

**Progesterone-only pills, hormonal IUDs, and implants.** The levonorgestrel IUD (Mirena, Liletta) releases roughly 20 mcg/day levonorgestrel locally. Systemic absorption is low but detectable. Progestin metabolites may appear on the DUTCH depending on the specific progestin. The etonogestrel implant (Nexplanon) raises progestin metabolites and suppresses LH/FSH, again distorting endogenous hormone assessment.

**GnRH agonists (leuprolide, buserelin) and GnRH antagonists (elagolix, relugolix).** These agents are used for endometriosis, uterine fibroids, and ART cycles. They suppress estradiol to castrate range within two to four weeks. A DUTCH test while on a GnRH agonist will show near-undetectable estrogen metabolites. This is pharmacologically expected, not pathological. Relugolix (Myfembree, combined with estradiol/norethindrone) partially restores estrogen but still suppresses endogenous production [18](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34670668/).

**Aromatase inhibitors (letrozole, anastrozole).** Used for estrogen receptor-positive breast cancer, ovulation induction in PCOS, and off-label fertility treatment. They block the conversion of androgens to estrogens. A woman on letrozole 2.5 mg will show very low estrogen metabolites and raised androgen metabolites on the DUTCH because the substrate testosterone and androstenedione are no longer being aromatized [19](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24470595/).

**Metformin.** Metformin modestly lowers DHEA-S and androgen metabolites in women with PCOS, likely through improved insulin sensitivity reducing ovarian androgen production [20](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10889833/). The effect is gradual (months, not days) and clinically meaningful when monitoring PCOS androgen load over time.

**Spironolactone.** Spironolactone blocks androgen receptors and inhibits adrenal 17-hydroxylase, reducing testosterone and DHEA metabolites on the DUTCH. Women using spironolactone for hormonal acne, PCOS-related hirsutism, or female-pattern hair loss should expect substantially lower androgen metabolite readings. This is the intended drug effect, not an artifact.

**Metyrapone (and ketoconazole).** These inhibit cortisol synthesis. Cortisol metabolites on the DUTCH will be low, while ACTH-driven precursors (11-deoxycortisol) accumulate. Not a common medication, but worth flagging for women being evaluated for Cushing's syndrome or on these agents for other reasons.

### Herbal and Dietary Factors

- **Cruciferous vegetables** consumed in large amounts increase 2-hydroxyestrone, shifting the 2:16 ratio upward. A week of intensive juicing before the DUTCH can meaningfully alter this ratio.
- **Alcohol** at more than seven drinks per week raises total urinary estrogen metabolites by impairing hepatic phase I estrogen metabolism.
- **High-dose vitamin C** (above 2,000 mg/day) can falsely raise or suppress cortisol on some platforms; stopping two days before the test is reasonable.

## How Your Life Stage Changes Drug Interference

The drugs that cause most trouble depend on where you are in your reproductive life.

**Reproductive years and trying to conceive.** If you are cycling and trying to understand your hormonal pattern for fertility purposes, even a single dose of Plan B (levonorgestrel 1.5 mg) taken in the prior 10 days will suppress progesterone metabolites and can delay the luteal phase timing needed for an accurate DUTCH collection. Tell your clinician about any recent hormonal exposure. Women with PCOS are particularly vulnerable to misinterpretation: their androgen metabolites are elevated at baseline, so metformin, spironolactone, or inositol supplements can all shift values enough to cross reference thresholds.

**Perimenopause.** The most common scenario: a woman starts low-dose transdermal estradiol (0.025 mg patch) for hot flashes and then gets a DUTCH test to "check her levels." The DUTCH will show elevated estrogen metabolites, not because she is over-dosed, but because the test captures both endogenous and exogenous estrogen breakdown products. Serum estradiol and symptom response are better guides to dosing during HRT than DUTCH estrogen metabolites [4](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30803772/).

**Postmenopause on HRT.** The interpretive gap is widest here. All estrogen metabolites come from exogenous sources. The 2:16 ratio may still offer theoretical information about metabolite pathway preference on HRT, but no randomized trial has shown that adjusting dose based on DUTCH 2:16 ratio in postmenopausal women on HRT improves clinical outcomes. That evidence gap is real and should be stated plainly.

## Pregnancy and Lactation: DUTCH Test Safety and Interpretation

**Pregnancy.** The DUTCH test is not validated for pregnancy. Progesterone, estriol, and cortisol all rise dramatically during gestation as part of normal placental physiology. Placental production of progesterone alone reaches 250 to 350 mg/day by the third trimester, compared with a luteal phase peak of roughly 25 mg/day [21](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16210377/). No reference ranges exist for pregnant women on this panel. Running a DUTCH test during pregnancy will produce uninterpretable results and does not assist clinical management. If you are pregnant and concerned about hormone-related symptoms, your OB or MFM should order targeted serum tests (progesterone, TSH, cortisol) with pregnancy-specific reference intervals.

**Drugs during pregnancy that affect hormones.** Any drug that distorts DUTCH results (topical progesterone, DHEA, glucocorticoids) carries its own pregnancy safety profile that is entirely separate from the lab question. Oral micronized progesterone is used clinically in early pregnancy for luteal phase support and in some trials for preterm birth prevention; it does not require a DUTCH to monitor in that context. DHEA supplementation lacks adequate safety data in pregnancy and should be stopped. Spironolactone is contraindicated in pregnancy due to feminization risk in male fetuses [22](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance).

**Lactation.** Prolactin suppresses GnRH pulsatility during lactation, keeping estrogen low and progesterone low. A DUTCH test during active breastfeeding will show a hormonal pattern similar to postmenopause: low estrogen metabolites, low progesterone metabolites, and potentially an unusual cortisol pattern driven by sleep deprivation and feeding-cycle stress. The results are not clinically actionable for reproductive hormone assessment during this phase. Breastfeeding women considering DHEA or adaptogenic supplements should discuss with a lactation medicine provider before starting, as transfer data for most adaptogens are absent.

**Contraception requirement note.** Several drugs that appear on the DUTCH interference list are themselves teratogenic or carry pregnancy cautions. Spironolactone requires reliable contraception. Metformin is generally considered low-risk in pregnancy (used for GDM and PCOS-related pregnancy management) but should only be continued under obstetric guidance. Aromatase inhibitors are strictly contraindicated in pregnancy.

## How to Lower or Raise Specific DUTCH Test Values

This question usually comes from one of two places: a woman who received a result and wants to understand whether lifestyle changes will move it, or a woman preparing for retesting and wanting to optimize conditions. Both are reasonable.

### To Lower Elevated Estrogen Metabolites

- Stop exogenous estrogen at least four to eight weeks before repeat testing if the goal is to assess endogenous status.
- Increase dietary fiber to 25 to 35 g/day; fiber binds bile acids and reduces entero-hepatic recirculation of estrogen, measurably lowering total urinary estrogen metabolite load [23](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/2373403/).
- Reduce alcohol to fewer than three drinks per week.
- Support phase II hepatic glucuronidation with DIM (diindolylmethane) or cruciferous vegetables. Evidence for DIM specifically shifting the 2:16 ratio is mostly from small trials; a 12-week randomized crossover trial in premenopausal women showed DIM 108 mg/day significantly increased 2-hydroxyestrone [24](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15623462/).

### To Raise Low Estrogen Metabolites

- In postmenopause, low estrogen metabolites on the DUTCH reflect absent ovarian function. The clinical question is whether HRT is appropriate, not whether lifestyle will restore estrogen. That decision should follow the Menopause Society's 2023 position statement framework, which supports HRT for women under 60 or within 10 years of menopause onset who have no contraindications [25](https://menopause.org/professional-resources/for-clinicians).
- In reproductive-age women with low estrogen metabolites, evaluate for hypothalamic amenorrhea, premature ovarian insufficiency (POI), or excessive energy deficit. POI affects roughly 1% of women under 40 [26](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22626620/).

### To Lower Elevated Cortisol Pattern

- Ashwagandha 300 mg twice daily reduced morning cortisol by 27.9% versus placebo in an eight-week RCT [14](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23439798/). Stopping it before a DUTCH test is the relevant interference point.
- Phosphatidylserine 400 to 800 mg/day has blunted exercise-induced cortisol in small trials; evidence for baseline diurnal cortisol reduction is weaker.

### To Raise Low Cortisol Pattern

- Low DUTCH cortisol in the absence of known HPA pathology may reflect circadian disruption, chronic under-eating, or recent glucocorticoid use suppressing endogenous production. Addressing sleep timing and caloric adequacy is first-line before supplementation.
- Adrenal "support" supplements (glandulars, adaptogenic blends) contain variable amounts of exogenous adrenal tissue or active compounds that will directly shift DUTCH cortisol values and should be stopped at least two weeks before testing.

## Who This Test Is and Is Not Right For, by Life Stage

**Good candidates:**

- Cycling women with suspected luteal phase deficiency wanting a functional progesterone metabolite assessment to complement serum day-21 progesterone
- Perimenopausal women with sleep disruption, mood changes, and fatigue who want a diurnal cortisol picture alongside sex hormones, understanding that the result is a functional snapshot, not a diagnostic certainty
- Women with PCOS not currently on COCs who want androgen metabolite profiling beyond total testosterone and free androgen index
- Postmenopausal women on compounded or topical HRT where serum monitoring is known to underestimate exposure, and where clinician and patient jointly want metabolite data [4](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30803772/)
- Women with suspected Cushing's syndrome or hypocortisolism as an adjunct to, not replacement for, 24-hour urinary free cortisol and late-night salivary cortisol (the Endocrine Society's preferred screening tools) [27](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25785136/)

**Poor candidates or situations requiring caution:**

- Women currently on COCs or any systemic hormonal contraception (results reflect drug, not endogenous hormones)
- Pregnant women (no reference ranges; results uninterpretable)
- Actively breastfeeding women seeking reproductive hormone assessment
- Women on GnRH agonists/antagonists for endometriosis or fibroids (profound suppression masks endogenous picture)
- Women seeking a standalone breast cancer risk assessment based on the 2:16 estrogen ratio (not validated for this purpose by ACOG or the Endocrine Society) [3](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/committee-opinion/articles/2017/12/hormone-therapy-in-primary-ovarian-insufficiency)

## How to Prepare for an Accurate DUTCH Test

Your clinician should review your full medication and supplement list at least two weeks before scheduling the collection. The minimum washout suggestions below apply when the goal is assessing endogenous hormone status:

| Substance | Suggested washout before testing |
|---|---|
| COCs, progestin-only pills | 3 months (full HPA/HPG recovery) |
| Topical estradiol or progesterone | 4 to 8 weeks |
| Oral progesterone (Prometrium) | 2 weeks |
| DHEA supplements | 2 weeks |
| Oral prednisone/hydrocortisone | 2 to 4 weeks (taper under supervision) |
| Inhaled corticosteroids | Discuss with clinician; do not stop without guidance |
| Licorice root | 2 weeks |
| Ashwagandha, adrenal glandulars | 2 weeks |
| High-dose biotin (&gt;10 mg/day) | 3 to 5 days |
| Spironolactone | 2 to 4 weeks |
| Aromatase inhibitors | Testing during treatment reflects drug effect; washout only under oncology supervision |

If you are on a medication that cannot safely be stopped, note that on your test requisition form. Your clinician should interpret the result in context of current drug exposure, not as if you are drug-free.

Cycle timing for premenopausal women: collect on days 19 to 22 of a 28-day cycle, or seven days before expected menstrual onset if your cycle is shorter or longer. First morning cortisol strip should be collected within 15 minutes of waking.
