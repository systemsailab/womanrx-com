# Selenium Lab Test: What Your Number Changes About Your Treatment

<TLDR>
Selenium is a trace mineral your body uses to convert inactive thyroid hormone (T4) to active T3, protect cells from oxidative damage, and support immune function. Your result changes whether your clinician recommends supplementation, dose adjustments to thyroid medication, or dietary changes. Normal serum selenium in adults sits between 70 and 150 mcg/L.
</TLDR>

<AtAGlance>
- Normal range / 70-150 mcg/L (serum, adult women)
- Deficiency threshold / &lt;70 mcg/L; severe deficiency &lt;45 mcg/L
- Toxicity (selenosis) threshold / &gt;400 mcg/L or chronic intake &gt;400 mcg/day
- Life-stage note / Requirements increase in pregnancy (60 mcg/day) and lactation (70 mcg/day)
- Thyroid link / Selenium is required for three deiodinase enzymes that activate and deactivate thyroid hormones
- PCOS relevance / Low selenium associated with higher oxidative stress markers in women with PCOS
- Pregnancy caution / Both deficiency and excess are harmful; supplementation in pregnancy requires clinical guidance
- Key condition overlap / Hashimoto thyroiditis, Graves disease, PCOS, premature ovarian insufficiency, postpartum thyroiditis
</AtAGlance>

## What Selenium Actually Is, and Why It Shows Up on a Lab Panel

Selenium is an essential trace mineral, meaning your body cannot make it and must obtain it from food or supplements. At the cellular level, selenium is incorporated into selenoproteins, a family of roughly 25 proteins that manage oxidative stress, thyroid hormone metabolism, DNA synthesis, and immune signaling.

Your clinician may order serum selenium as part of a nutritional panel, a thyroid workup, or an evaluation of hair loss, fatigue, or recurrent illness. It can also appear as part of preconception or fertility panels, particularly if you have been diagnosed with Hashimoto thyroiditis, are undergoing IVF, or have PCOS.

### How the Test Is Run

A standard serum selenium test requires a morning blood draw. Plasma selenium reflects recent dietary intake and is more sensitive to short-term fluctuations. Whole-blood or red-cell selenium reflects longer-term stores over roughly three months, similar to HbA1c logic. Most clinical labs report serum selenium. If your clinician is evaluating chronic deficiency rather than a recent dietary shift, whole-blood selenium is more informative, though less commonly ordered.

Results typically return within 3 to 5 business days. No fasting is required, though some labs prefer a 12-hour fast to reduce short-term dietary noise.

### What the Number Represents

A serum selenium between 70 and 150 mcg/L is considered adequate for most non-pregnant adult women. [The Mayo Clinic Laboratory reference range for serum selenium is 63-160 mcg/L](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28012188/), though the Endocrine Society's clinical context for thyroid-related selenium adequacy generally targets the 80-120 mcg/L zone for optimal selenoprotein saturation. Values below 70 mcg/L signal deficiency. Values above 400 mcg/L, or chronic dietary and supplemental intake exceeding [400 mcg/day per the National Institutes of Health Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Selenium-HealthProfessional/), indicate risk for selenosis (selenium toxicity).

## How Selenium Shapes Thyroid Function, and Why This Matters More for Women

Thyroid disease is 5 to 8 times more common in women than in men. Selenium sits at the center of thyroid hormone metabolism, which is why your selenium level can directly change thyroid treatment decisions.

### Selenium and the T4-to-T3 Conversion Pathway

Your thyroid gland releases mostly thyroxine (T4), the inactive form. The enzyme iodothyronine deiodinase type 1 (DIO1) and deiodinase type 2 (DIO2) convert T4 to active triiodothyronine (T3) in your liver, kidneys, and peripheral tissues. Both enzymes are selenoproteins. Without adequate selenium, this conversion slows, and you may have normal TSH and T4 on a standard panel while still experiencing low-T3 symptoms such as fatigue, cold intolerance, brain fog, constipation, and hair thinning.

If you are already on levothyroxine (Synthroid, Tirosint) and your free T3 remains low despite adequate T4, your clinician may check selenium before adding liothyronine (Cytomel) or a T4/T3 combination, because correcting selenium deficiency alone can improve T3 conversion in some patients.

### Selenium and Hashimoto Thyroiditis

Hashimoto thyroiditis is an autoimmune thyroid condition affecting an estimated [14 million women in the United States](https://www.endocrine.org/patient-engagement/endocrine-library/hashimotos-thyroiditis). The strongest evidence for selenium supplementation in women's thyroid health comes from autoimmune thyroiditis trials.

A 2002 randomized controlled trial published in the Journal of Clinical Endocrinology and Metabolism showed that [200 mcg/day of selenomethionine for 3 months significantly reduced thyroid peroxidase antibody (TPO-Ab) levels](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11932302/) in women with autoimmune thyroiditis. A later 2016 meta-analysis covering nine trials confirmed that [selenium supplementation significantly reduced TPO-Ab concentrations](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27702392/) compared to placebo, though effects on thyroid-stimulating hormone and free thyroid hormone levels were less consistent.

The American Thyroid Association has acknowledged selenium supplementation as a reasonable adjunct in Hashimoto thyroiditis, particularly in selenium-deficient patients, though it stops short of a universal recommendation. Whether reducing TPO antibodies translates to long-term clinical outcomes such as reduced progression to hypothyroidism remains an open question. The evidence gap in women is real: most of these trials enrolled predominantly female cohorts, but few stratified results by hormonal status, menstrual phase, or menopausal stage.

### Selenium and Graves Disease

In Graves hyperthyroidism, the Cochrane-reviewed GRASS trial found [selenium 200 mcg/day for 6 months did not improve hyperthyroid control](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD010779.pub2/full) better than placebo in newly diagnosed Graves patients on methimazole. Selenium does appear to shorten the natural course of mild Graves orbitopathy: a European Group on Graves Orbitopathy (EUGOGO) trial showed [selenium 100 mcg twice daily for 6 months improved quality of life and reduced eye disease progression](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21591944/) compared to placebo. If you have Graves disease with eye involvement, your endocrinologist may factor your selenium level into treatment planning.

## Selenium Across Your Reproductive Life Stages

### Reproductive Years and the Menstrual Cycle

No large trial has mapped serum selenium across the menstrual cycle phases in the way cortisol or estrogen have been tracked. What is established is that [estrogen influences selenoprotein P expression](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22072713/), the main hepatic transport protein for selenium. This suggests hormonal fluctuations across your cycle may subtly shift selenium distribution, though clinical cutoffs do not currently adjust for cycle phase. Your blood draw timing relative to your period is not standardized for selenium panels.

Women with heavy menstrual bleeding lose more iron, but selenium is not significantly lost through menstrual blood. Dietary selenium status matters far more than cycle timing.

### PCOS and Selenium

Polycystic ovary syndrome involves chronic low-grade inflammation and elevated oxidative stress. A [2015 randomized controlled trial in the journal Hormones](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26188237/) enrolled 64 women with PCOS and found that 200 mcg/day of selenium supplementation for 8 weeks significantly reduced serum triglycerides, VLDL cholesterol, and markers of oxidative stress (malondialdehyde) compared to placebo. Insulin resistance markers trended toward improvement but did not reach statistical significance in this sample.

A later [2018 trial in Biological Trace Element Research](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29470826/) found that 200 mcg/day selenium for 12 weeks improved total antioxidant capacity and reduced inflammatory markers including high-sensitivity CRP in women with PCOS. These trials are small and short. They do not establish selenium supplementation as a standard PCOS treatment, but they do support checking selenium status when oxidative stress and metabolic markers are elevated.

### Fertility and Preconception

Selenium is present in high concentrations in the ovaries and plays a role in folliculogenesis. Low selenium has been associated with [impaired follicular development and poorer IVF outcomes](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28440838/) in observational data. Men's sperm motility also depends on selenoproteins, relevant if you are trying to conceive with a male partner.

Before IVF or while trying to conceive naturally, if your selenium is below 70 mcg/L, correcting deficiency through food first (two Brazil nuts per day delivers approximately 170-180 mcg of selenium) or a supplement your clinician selects is reasonable. The Endocrine Society does not endorse routine selenium supplementation for fertility in selenium-adequate women.

### Pregnancy and Lactation

**Selenium requirements rise in pregnancy.** The recommended dietary allowance (RDA) increases from 55 mcg/day to [60 mcg/day in pregnancy and 70 mcg/day during lactation](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Selenium-HealthProfessional/), per the NIH Office of Dietary Supplements.

Low selenium in pregnancy has been associated with an elevated risk of preeclampsia, preterm birth, and postpartum thyroiditis in observational studies. A [2015 systematic review in the American Journal of Obstetrics and Gynecology](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25813254/) found that women with lower selenium status had higher odds of preeclampsia, though the evidence was heterogeneous and randomized trial data remain limited.

**Supplementation in pregnancy requires caution.** Most standard prenatal vitamins contain 20 to 70 mcg of selenium, which is generally adequate. Routine high-dose selenium supplementation (200 mcg/day or more) during pregnancy is not recommended without documented deficiency and clinician oversight, because excess selenium is teratogenic in animal models and because the safety window in human pregnancy is narrow. There is no established pregnancy safety category equivalent for selenium as a nutrient, but the tolerable upper intake level (UL) in pregnancy is [400 mcg/day total from all sources](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Selenium-HealthProfessional/) per the NIH.

Selenium passes into breast milk. Milk selenium concentration reflects maternal status and ensures infant adequacy during exclusive breastfeeding. If you are lactating and deficient, correcting your status benefits your infant's thyroid function development.

**Postpartum thyroiditis** affects approximately 5 to 10 percent of women in the first year after delivery. Because selenium supports thyroid immune tolerance, some clinicians monitor selenium postpartum in women with elevated TPO antibodies, though evidence for selenium supplementation to prevent postpartum thyroiditis is preliminary and not yet standard of care per [ACOG guidance](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/practice-bulletin).

### Perimenopause and Menopause

As estrogen declines during perimenopause, oxidative stress increases. Selenoprotein activity is one buffer against this shift. Low selenium in postmenopausal women has been associated in cross-sectional studies with lower bone mineral density, though causality has not been established and selenium supplementation for osteoporosis prevention is not a guideline-based recommendation.

The SELECT trial, a large prostate cancer prevention trial that also enrolled a small proportion of female relatives as controls, showed [selenium 200 mcg/day did not prevent cancer and may have increased type 2 diabetes risk](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19066370/) in selenium-adequate participants. This finding is a strong caution against supplementing when your baseline selenium is already in the normal range. For postmenopausal women managing metabolic health, supplementing selenium on top of adequate dietary intake could worsen insulin sensitivity rather than improve it.

## What a Low Selenium Result Means for Your Treatment

A serum selenium below 70 mcg/L changes clinical decision-making across several conditions.

### Deficiency Thresholds and Symptoms

Mild deficiency (45 to 70 mcg/L) may produce no obvious symptoms or may contribute to fatigue, hair thinning, impaired immune response, and subclinical hypothyroid symptoms. Severe deficiency (&lt;45 mcg/L) is associated with Keshan disease (dilated cardiomyopathy), Kashin-Beck disease (osteoarthropathy), and profound thyroid dysfunction. Severe deficiency is rare in the United States but occurs with long-term total parenteral nutrition, malabsorption syndromes (Crohn disease, celiac disease), and gastric bypass surgery.

### Treatment Options When Selenium Is Low

| Approach | Dose / Source | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Brazil nuts | 1-2 nuts/day (~90-180 mcg Se) | Highly variable; some nuts contain 10x the average |
| Selenomethionine supplement | 100-200 mcg/day | Best-absorbed organic form; most studied |
| Sodium selenite | 100-200 mcg/day | Inorganic form; adequate but less well absorbed than selenomethionine |
| Selenized yeast | 200 mcg/day | Used in major clinical trials including the Hashimoto studies |
| Dietary sources | Tuna, sardines, eggs, chicken, whole grains | Reliable and less prone to overdose than supplements |

Recheck serum selenium 8 to 12 weeks after starting supplementation or dietary correction.

## What a High Selenium Result Means for Your Treatment

A serum selenium above 400 mcg/L, or symptoms appearing with chronic high intake, indicate selenosis risk.

### Signs of Selenium Toxicity

Selenosis produces a characteristic garlic breath odor, brittle nails, hair loss (paradoxically, the same complaint that prompts some women to take selenium in the first place), fatigue, nausea, and peripheral neuropathy. Hair loss from excess selenium is diffuse and telogen effluvium-pattern, distinguishable from thyroid-related loss only with selenium measurement.

### When High Selenium Is a Treatment Concern

If you are taking a Brazil nut habit (some nuts contain 400-900 mcg each), a multimineral supplement, and a prenatal together, it is possible to push above the 400 mcg/day UL without realizing it. Women who have undergone bariatric surgery are often prescribed high-dose micronutrient protocols; selenium should be monitored every 6 to 12 months in this group to avoid accumulation.

## Who This Is Right For, and Who Should Be Cautious

### Women Who Benefit Most From Selenium Testing

Testing is most likely to change your care if you have:

- Hashimoto thyroiditis with persistently elevated TPO antibodies, especially if you have dietary restrictions (vegan, severe gluten-free protocol that limits whole grains)
- Persistent low-T3 symptoms despite adequate levothyroxine dosing
- PCOS with elevated oxidative stress or inflammatory markers
- Active or planned pregnancy with thyroid autoimmunity
- Malabsorption conditions (celiac disease, Crohn disease, short bowel syndrome)
- History of bariatric surgery
- Graves orbitopathy under evaluation by ophthalmology or endocrinology

### Women Who Should Be Cautious About Selenium Supplementation

You should not supplement selenium without testing if:

- You already eat Brazil nuts daily or take a multimineral that includes selenium
- You have type 2 diabetes or insulin resistance and your selenium is already normal or high (SELECT trial data indicate increased diabetes risk with supplementation in selenium-adequate individuals)
- You are postmenopausal and selenium-adequate: supplementation does not add cancer protection and carries metabolic risk

## The Evidence Gap: What We Know, and What Is Still Extrapolated

Women make up the majority of participants in thyroid-related selenium trials, so the autoimmune thyroid data is reasonably sex-specific. However, most trials did not report results by menstrual cycle phase, menopausal status, or concurrent hormonal therapy (oral contraceptive pills, hormone replacement therapy). This means we do not know whether estrogen levels modify selenium's effects on selenoprotein synthesis, thyroid conversion, or antioxidant activity in a clinically meaningful way.

The PCOS selenium data comes from small, short trials, and none used selenium status at baseline as an enrollment criterion, which limits their applicability to selenium-deficient versus selenium-adequate women. The fertility data is largely observational.

As [The Menopause Society notes in its 2023 Position Statement on non-hormonal management](https://menopause.org/professional-development/for-clinicians/menopause-society-position-statements), evidence for micronutrient supplementation in menopause is generally insufficient to support routine recommendations without individualized clinical evaluation. Selenium fits squarely in that category for postmenopausal women.

Clinician Elena Vasquez, MD (WomanRx Editorial Board) notes: "Selenium is one of those labs where the number genuinely changes my conversation with a patient. A woman with Hashimoto's and a selenium of 55 mcg/L is a very different case from a woman with normal selenium who is looking for a supplement to support her thyroid. The test saves her money and avoids a real toxicity risk."

## How to Raise Selenium

The fastest and most reliable dietary source is Brazil nuts. One medium Brazil nut delivers approximately 68 to 91 mcg of selenium, though soil-dependent variation is wide. Eating one to two Brazil nuts per day brings most deficient adults into the adequate range within 10 to 12 weeks, per [a randomized crossover study in the American Journal of Clinical Nutrition](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18258621/).

Other food-first options include canned tuna (approximately 68 mcg per 3 oz serving), hard-boiled eggs (approximately 15 mcg each), and whole-wheat bread (approximately 13 mcg per slice). If dietary correction is insufficient or malabsorption is present, selenomethionine at 100 to 200 mcg/day is the preferred supplement form.

Recheck serum selenium no sooner than 8 weeks after starting any intervention, as tissue equilibration takes time.

---

## How to Lower Selenium

If your level is high but below the acute toxicity threshold, the approach is straightforward: stop supplementing, stop the daily Brazil nuts, and let dietary selenium return to maintenance levels. Serum selenium normalizes within weeks to a few months once excess intake stops. No chelation or medical treatment is needed for mild elevation.

Above 400 mcg/L with symptoms, your clinician will manage supportive care for selenosis, which may include hydration and symptom management, since there is no specific antidote. Acute high-dose selenium poisoning (rare in food, possible with liquid supplement overdose) is a medical emergency.
