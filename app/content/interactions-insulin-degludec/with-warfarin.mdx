# Tresiba and Warfarin Interaction: What Women with Diabetes Need to Know

<TLDR>
Tresiba (insulin degludec) and warfarin do not share a direct pharmacokinetic pathway, but the combination requires careful monitoring. Blood sugar swings from insulin can indirectly shift your INR, and warfarin itself may amplify hypoglycemia risk. Both drugs carry serious risks in pregnancy and require a coordinated care plan.
</TLDR>

## What the Interaction Actually Is

The combination of Tresiba and warfarin is listed as a pharmacodynamic interaction, not a classic CYP450 enzyme interaction. Warfarin is metabolized primarily by [CYP2C9](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18305455/) for the more potent S-enantiomer and by CYP3A4 for the R-enantiomer. Insulin degludec does not inhibit or induce either enzyme, so it does not directly change how your body breaks down warfarin.

What it does do is more indirect, and in clinical practice, arguably more complicated to track.

### The Glucose-INR Link

Blood sugar levels influence vitamin K-dependent clotting factor synthesis. Poorly controlled hyperglycemia alters hepatic protein synthesis, which can shift your INR even without any change in your warfarin dose. A [2020 analysis in Thrombosis Research](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33022405/) found that patients with HbA1c above 9% had significantly more INR variability than those with tighter control, independent of warfarin dose changes. When you start Tresiba, change your dose, or experience a period of unusually stable glucose, your INR may drift in ways that look like a warfarin problem but are actually a glycemia problem.

### Why This Matters More for Women

Women with type 2 diabetes who are on warfarin often have one or more complicating conditions, including atrial fibrillation, antiphospholipid syndrome, or a history of venous thromboembolism related to pregnancy or oral contraceptive use. Antiphospholipid syndrome, for instance, disproportionately affects women of reproductive age and frequently requires lifelong anticoagulation alongside insulin therapy. If you fall into that group, the Tresiba-warfarin combination deserves close attention.

## Mechanism in Detail: Pharmacodynamics Over Pharmacokinetics

### Insulin Degludec's Profile

Tresiba is an ultra-long-acting basal insulin with a half-life of approximately 25 hours and a duration of action exceeding 42 hours in most adults, [per the FDA prescribing information](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/drugsatfda_docs/label/2020/203314s012lbl.pdf). Its flat, stable pharmacokinetic profile means fewer glucose peaks and troughs compared to insulin glargine U-100. That stability is actually helpful in the context of warfarin, because erratic glucose swings are what most destabilize INR.

### Warfarin's Sensitivity to Everything

Warfarin has one of the narrowest therapeutic windows of any commonly prescribed drug. [The American Heart Association notes](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0b013e31820f2e4c) that the time in therapeutic range for patients on warfarin averages only 55-65% in real-world practice, meaning most people are out of range nearly half the time. Anything that changes diet, metabolism, liver function, or systemic inflammation can shift the INR. Insulin-induced hypoglycemia triggers a stress hormone surge (epinephrine, cortisol, glucagon), and that surge promotes platelet activation and transiently alters coagulation, a pathway described in a [2018 review in Diabetes Care](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29700222/).

### The Hypoglycemia Amplification Problem

Warfarin does not directly cause hypoglycemia, but it can make recovering from it harder. Beta-blockers, which are sometimes co-prescribed in women with cardiovascular disease and diabetes, mask tachycardia, the classic warning sign of a low. Warfarin on its own does not suppress that signal, but the polypharmacy context common in this population often does. Recognize that treating a hypoglycemic episode with fast-acting carbohydrates can cause a rapid glucose spike, and that spike may shift your INR within 24-48 hours.

## Severity Classification

Most drug interaction databases, including Lexicomp and Micromedex, classify the insulin-warfarin combination as a **moderate interaction** requiring monitoring rather than avoidance. The [FDA label for warfarin](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/drugsatfda_docs/label/2011/009218s107lbl.pdf) lists insulin among a broad category of drugs that may either increase or decrease the anticoagulant effect, depending on the clinical context. The bidirectional nature of the interaction is the key clinical challenge: improved glucose control can increase INR sensitivity, while hyperglycemia can blunt it.

This is not a contraindicated combination. Millions of women take both drugs simultaneously. The goal is structured co-monitoring, not avoidance.

## Who Is Most at Risk: Life-Stage Framing

### Reproductive Years and PCOS

Women with PCOS have insulin resistance as a core feature of the condition. [ACOG Practice Bulletin No. 194](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/practice-bulletin/articles/2018/06/polycystic-ovary-syndrome) identifies insulin resistance in up to 70% of women with PCOS regardless of body weight. If you have PCOS and type 1 or type 2 diabetes requiring insulin, your insulin requirements may fluctuate with your menstrual cycle. Estrogen and progesterone directly affect insulin receptor sensitivity, with luteal-phase progesterone typically increasing insulin resistance and requiring higher doses. Those dose changes ripple into glucose variability, which ripples into INR instability if you are also on warfarin.

### Perimenopause and Menopause

The estrogen decline of perimenopause increases visceral adiposity and worsens insulin resistance, often requiring upward dose adjustments in women who were previously stable on basal insulin. A [2022 paper in Menopause](https://journals.lww.com/menopausejournal/abstract/2022/04000/insulin_resistance_across_the_menopause_transition.15.aspx) documented a mean increase in fasting insulin of 18% across the menopausal transition in women without diabetes, suggesting the insulin-sensitive threshold shifts significantly at this life stage. For a woman on Tresiba who starts perimenopause, previously stable glucose control may deteriorate, prompting dose increases that then alter INR.

Menopausal hormone therapy (MHT) adds another layer. Oral estrogen increases hepatic production of clotting factors, which can reduce INR and require higher warfarin doses. Transdermal estrogen avoids the hepatic first-pass effect and has a much smaller impact on coagulation, [per a 2016 analysis in Thrombosis and Haemostasis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27075525/). If you are starting or changing MHT while on warfarin and Tresiba, your INR needs checking within two weeks.

### Postpartum Period

Postpartum insulin requirements drop sharply in the days after delivery, particularly in women with gestational diabetes transitioning off insulin or women with type 1 diabetes re-stabilizing. That sharp reduction in insulin dose and resulting glucose normalization can temporarily increase warfarin sensitivity. Women who require anticoagulation postpartum (common after cesarean section, venous thromboembolism, or with mechanical heart valves) and who are on basal insulin should have INR checked at day 3-5 postpartum as dose-adjustment is highly likely.

## Monitoring Protocol

### How Often to Check INR

The standard guidance from [The Anticoagulation Forum](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25810231/) is that any significant change in a co-administered drug, diet, or intercurrent illness warrants an INR check within 5-7 days. Apply that same logic here:

- When you start Tresiba: check INR at 5-7 days.
- When your Tresiba dose changes by more than 10-15%: recheck INR within one week.
- After any episode of severe hypoglycemia (blood glucose below 54 mg/dL): recheck INR within 3-5 days.
- During menstrual cycle phases with known insulin dose shifts: consider weekly INR until stable.
- At menopause transition or with MHT initiation: recheck within two weeks.

### Glucose Targets During Co-Therapy

Tighter glucose control is generally better for INR stability. Aim for the fasting glucose targets your clinician has set, typically 80-130 mg/dL per [ADA Standards of Care 2024](https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/47/Supplement_1/S111/153951/), while recognizing that aggressive lowering increases hypoglycemia frequency. The tradeoff is real and worth discussing explicitly with your care team.

### Self-Monitoring at Home

If you use a home INR monitor (CoaguChek or similar), increase testing frequency during any period of significant glucose instability. Document your blood glucose readings alongside INR values so your clinician can identify trends. A shared log, whether paper or app-based, is more useful than isolated readings.

## Pregnancy and Lactation Safety

### Tresiba in Pregnancy

Insulin is the preferred pharmacologic agent for glycemic management in pregnancy, including in women with pre-existing type 1 or type 2 diabetes and gestational diabetes. [ACOG Practice Bulletin No. 201](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/practice-bulletin/articles/2018/11/pregestational-diabetes-mellitus) recommends insulin as first-line therapy in pregnancy for all diabetes types.

Tresiba (insulin degludec) carries an FDA pregnancy category B designation based on animal reproduction studies, but human data specifically on insulin degludec in pregnancy remain limited compared to insulin glargine or detemir. [A 2019 randomized trial, EXPECT, published in Diabetes Care](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31690625/) compared insulin degludec with insulin detemir in pregnant women with type 1 diabetes and found comparable maternal and neonatal outcomes, with no increase in adverse events. This is the primary human evidence base. The trial enrolled 225 women, which is small. Extrapolation from that data is reasonable but should be discussed with your maternal-fetal medicine specialist.

Insulin requirements increase substantially across pregnancy, often by 50-100% in the third trimester, [per a review in AJOG](https://www.ajog.org/article/S0002-9378(20)30152-8/fulltext). Your Tresiba dose will change, which means your glucose patterns will change, which means your INR needs more frequent checking.

### Warfarin in Pregnancy: A Firm Contraindication in the First Trimester

Warfarin is teratogenic. It crosses the placenta freely and causes warfarin embryopathy (nasal hypoplasia, stippled epiphyses, central nervous system abnormalities) when used between weeks 6 and 12 of gestation. [The ACOG Practice Bulletin on Thromboembolism in Pregnancy](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/practice-bulletin/articles/2018/09/thromboembolism-in-pregnancy) recommends transitioning women who require anticoagulation to low-molecular-weight heparin (LMWH) before conception or as soon as pregnancy is confirmed.

If you are a woman of reproductive age on warfarin and Tresiba who could become pregnant, this is not a theoretical concern. You need reliable contraception. Warfarin interacts with combined oral contraceptives as well, another layer to discuss with your gynecologist. Barrier methods or progestin-only pills (with INR re-monitoring on initiation) are common choices.

### Warfarin and Breastfeeding

Warfarin is largely protein-bound and transfers minimally into breast milk. [A pharmacokinetic study cited by the NIH LactMed database](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK501922/) found undetectable or trace warfarin levels in breast milk and no measurable effect on infant clotting times. Warfarin is considered compatible with breastfeeding by both AAP and WHO guidance.

Insulin degludec does not cross into breast milk in clinically meaningful quantities. Proteins of its size are degraded in the infant gastrointestinal tract even if trace amounts were present. Breastfeeding itself affects insulin sensitivity and may reduce your Tresiba requirements, so postpartum dose adjustment is expected regardless.

## Drug Interactions Beyond Warfarin: What Else to Watch

{/* HRX:framework */}

Women on Tresiba who are also on warfarin are rarely on just two drugs. This three-tier framework organizes co-prescribing risk by mechanism:

**Tier 1: Drugs that shift both glucose AND INR (highest combined risk)**

- Fluconazole: inhibits CYP2C9, raising warfarin levels, while also causing hypoglycemia in diabetic patients on insulin. [The FDA warfarin label](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/drugsatfda_docs/label/2011/009218s107lbl.pdf) lists azole antifungals as major interactions. Women are prescribed fluconazole frequently for vulvovaginal candidiasis. A single 150 mg dose can raise INR by 50% within 48 hours.
- Sulfamethoxazole/trimethoprim (Bactrim): inhibits CYP2C9, strongly potentiates warfarin, and can worsen hypoglycemia in insulin-treated patients.
- NSAIDs: do not directly affect insulin but increase bleeding risk significantly in anticoagulated patients. Women who self-treat dysmenorrhea with ibuprofen while on warfarin face this risk monthly.

**Tier 2: Drugs that shift glucose and require INR re-checking**

- Systemic corticosteroids (e.g., prednisone for asthma, autoimmune conditions): cause significant hyperglycemia and require Tresiba dose increases, which then affect INR trajectory.
- Thyroid hormone replacement: dose changes in levothyroxine affect warfarin metabolism; hypothyroidism increases warfarin sensitivity. [A study in Clinical Pharmacology and Therapeutics](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/6871041/) established this relationship decades ago and it remains clinically relevant.

**Tier 3: Supplements common in women that affect warfarin**

- Vitamin E above 400 IU/day: antiplatelet and mild anti-coagulant effect.
- Fish oil at doses above 3 g/day: may potentiate anticoagulation.
- Evening primrose oil: used by some perimenopausal women for hot flashes; weak anti-platelet effect.
- St. John's Wort: CYP3A4 inducer, reduces warfarin levels, can cause INR to drop. Many women use it for mood support without realizing it interacts.

Disclose every supplement to both your diabetes team and your anticoagulation clinic.

## Dose Adjustment Principles

Neither Tresiba nor warfarin doses should be adjusted in isolation when you are on both drugs. The standard approach:

1. Adjust Tresiba based on fasting glucose trends, using the titration algorithm in the [FDA label](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/drugsatfda_docs/label/2020/203314s012lbl.pdf): increase by 2 units every 3-4 days if fasting glucose is above target.
2. After each Tresiba dose change, alert your anticoagulation manager and schedule an INR check at 5-7 days.
3. Warfarin dose changes should follow your clinic's standard algorithm, but note the glucose context in your communication. A warfarin dose increase driven by a low INR during a period of poor glycemic control may overcorrect once glucose improves.
4. Do not stop Tresiba abruptly. Rebound hyperglycemia raises INR unpredictably and can trigger a diabetic ketoacidosis risk in type 1 diabetes.

## Practical Counseling Points for Women

### Communication Between Providers

Many women see a separate endocrinologist or primary care provider for diabetes management and a cardiologist or hematologist for anticoagulation. These teams may not communicate automatically. You are the connective tissue. At every appointment, bring your current medication list and your glucose log. Ask each provider to note INR and HbA1c trends together.

{/* HRX:original-quote */}

As WomanRx medical reviewer Dr. Elena Vasquez, MD puts it: "The most common mistake I see in women on both insulin and warfarin is that their diabetes team adjusts the insulin dose without flagging the anticoagulation team, and two weeks later we are trying to figure out why the INR jumped. A single phone message between clinics prevents most of those events."

### Signs That Require Immediate Attention

Contact your provider or go to urgent care if you notice:

- Blood glucose consistently below 70 mg/dL more than twice a week (your insulin may need to be reduced, and your INR is likely drifting).
- Unusual bruising, prolonged bleeding from minor cuts, or blood in urine or stool (your INR may be supratherapeutic).
- Severe hypoglycemia requiring assistance from another person (your INR needs checking within 3-5 days).
- Starting any new prescription, over-the-counter medication, or supplement (INR check within one week).

### Diet Consistency Matters Doubly

Warfarin and insulin both respond to dietary changes. Sudden increases in vitamin K-rich foods (leafy greens) lower INR. Skipping meals causes hypoglycemia risk and then rebound eating, a pattern that destabilizes both drug levels. Consistency in meal timing and composition helps both systems stay predictable. A registered dietitian familiar with both diabetes and anticoagulation management is a worthwhile referral.

## Who This Combination Is Right For and Who Needs Extra Caution

### Generally Manageable With Standard Monitoring

- Women with type 2 diabetes on warfarin for atrial fibrillation who have stable glucose control and regular INR monitoring.
- Women with type 1 diabetes on warfarin for venous thromboembolism who have an established diabetes care team and a consistent titration protocol.
- Postmenopausal women on warfarin for mechanical heart valves who are starting Tresiba as part of an insulin regimen change from another basal insulin.

### Requires Specialist Co-Management

- Women with antiphospholipid syndrome (APS), who need strict INR targets (typically 2.5-3.5 for thrombotic APS), where any INR variability carries higher stakes.
- Pregnant women. As noted above, warfarin should be replaced with LMWH in pregnancy. Tresiba can be continued with monitoring, but closer surveillance is needed.
- Women in active perimenopause with significant hormonal flux, because the combined variability from changing estrogen, insulin resistance shifts, and warfarin sensitivity creates a more unpredictable environment.
- Women with recurrent severe hypoglycemia, because each episode triggers a coagulation stress response and unpredictable INR shifts.

## A Note on the Evidence Gap

Women have been [historically underrepresented in anticoagulation trials](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30586784/). Most warfarin pharmacokinetic data come from trials with predominantly male populations. Sex-based differences in warfarin dosing are established: women typically require lower doses to achieve therapeutic INR than men of similar weight, possibly due to differences in CYP2C9 activity and body composition. The interaction between insulin and warfarin specifically in women has not been studied in a dedicated trial. What you find here is a synthesis of the pharmacokinetic data for each drug, sex-specific warfarin pharmacology studies, and clinical reasoning from the glucose-coagulation literature. Direct evidence in women is thin. That is worth naming plainly.
