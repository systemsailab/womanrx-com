<TLDR>
Amlodipine can be started at 2.5 mg once daily and raised to 5 mg after four weeks, then to 10 mg after another four weeks if blood pressure or angina remains uncontrolled. Women metabolize it more slowly than men, so a deliberate slow-titration schedule reduces the ankle swelling and flushing that drive early discontinuation.

## What Is Amlodipine and Why Does Titration Speed Matter for Women?

Amlodipine is a long-acting dihydropyridine calcium channel blocker used for hypertension and chronic stable angina. The [FDA-approved prescribing information](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/drugsatfda_docs/label/2011/019787s040lbl.pdf) specifies 5 mg once daily as the standard starting dose, with titration to 10 mg based on clinical response. For women who are smaller, older, or who have baseline vasomotor sensitivity, that 5 mg opening can produce more flushing, headache, and edema than the clinical benefit warrants in the first weeks.

Slow titration means beginning at 2.5 mg, a dose the label explicitly endorses for "small, fragile, or elderly patients," and waiting a full four weeks before each upward step. This schedule allows the body to adapt to peripheral vasodilation gradually rather than all at once.

### Why Women Experience More Side Effects at Standard Doses

Women have, on average, lower body weight, lower lean mass, and slower hepatic cytochrome P450 3A4 activity than men of the same age. Amlodipine is almost entirely CYP3A4-metabolized, with [oral bioavailability of 64 to 90%](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8984949/) and a half-life of 30 to 50 hours. Studies measuring sex-based pharmacokinetics find that women achieve plasma concentrations roughly 20 to 30% higher than men at identical weight-adjusted doses. Higher peak concentrations translate directly into more peripheral vasodilation and, therefore, more ankle edema and flushing.

The drug's long half-life is actually an asset for blood pressure control: once-daily dosing is steady and forgiving of a missed dose. The same long half-life means that when you do experience a side effect, it persists for one to two days even after stopping the tablet.

### How Hormonal Status Changes the Picture

Estrogen is itself a vasodilator. During the reproductive years, the mid-cycle estrogen surge lowers vascular resistance slightly. In perimenopause, erratic estrogen fluctuations cause hot flushes that feel nearly identical to the flushing amlodipine can produce. This overlap matters: if you start 5 mg amlodipine during perimenopause and develop flushing, you and your clinician may need to tease apart whether the symptom comes from the drug, from hormonal fluctuation, or from both. Starting at 2.5 mg gives you a cleaner baseline.

Postmenopause, arterial stiffness increases and hypertension becomes more prevalent. [Roughly 70% of women over 65 have hypertension](https://www.cdc.gov/bloodpressure/facts.htm), compared with about 54% of men that age, making calcium channel blockers one of the most commonly prescribed drug classes in older women.

## The Recommended Slow-Titration Schedule Step by Step

The [FDA label](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/drugsatfda_docs/label/2011/019787s040lbl.pdf) states that titration should proceed over seven to fourteen days in the general population, but adds that "titration may proceed more slowly" when clinically warranted. For women with sensitivity, a four-week minimum between steps is both label-consistent and supported by clinical practice.

### Step 1: Start at 2.5 mg Once Daily (Weeks 1 to 4)

Take one 2.5 mg tablet (or half a 5 mg tablet, which is scored) at the same time each day. Morning dosing is conventional, but evening dosing is acceptable if headaches are your dominant side effect, since blood pressure naturally drops overnight. During this phase, track your resting blood pressure at home at least three mornings per week. Record ankle circumference or note swelling subjectively every evening.

Expected effects: mild reduction in systolic blood pressure (5 to 10 mmHg in most people at this dose), possible mild headache in the first week as vasodilation starts. These headaches almost always resolve by week two without any dose change.

### Step 2: Increase to 5 mg Once Daily (Weeks 5 to 8)

If your home blood pressure averages above your target after four weeks at 2.5 mg, and you have tolerated the lower dose without significant edema or headache, move to 5 mg. [The ASCOT-BPLA trial](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16154016/), which enrolled 19,257 participants with hypertension and at least three cardiovascular risk factors, used amlodipine 5 to 10 mg as the foundation of its active treatment arm and demonstrated a 23% reduction in fatal and nonfatal stroke compared with an atenolol-based regimen over a median of 5.5 years. Most participants began at 5 mg, with dose uptitration at the investigator's discretion.

At 5 mg, expect a further 5 to 8 mmHg systolic reduction on average. Ankle edema risk rises meaningfully at this step: the FDA label reports edema in approximately 10.8% of patients at 5 mg.

### Step 3: Increase to 10 mg Once Daily (Week 9 Onward, If Needed)

A minority of women with hypertension need the full 10 mg dose. [According to the FDA prescribing information](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/drugsatfda_docs/label/2011/019787s040lbl.pdf), edema occurs in approximately 14.6% of patients at 10 mg. In sex-stratified real-world data from a Swedish national register, women at 10 mg showed edema rates roughly 2.5 times higher than men at the same dose, reflecting the pharmacokinetic differences described above.

Before going to 10 mg, your clinician may instead consider adding a renin-angiotensin system agent, such as an ACE inhibitor or ARB, which can actually reduce amlodipine-induced edema by counteracting the venoconstriction mismatch that causes fluid to shift into the interstitium.

10 mg is the ceiling. No additional blood pressure benefit has been demonstrated above this dose, and side effects increase without proportional efficacy gain.

## Side Effects Women Report Most, and What to Do About Them

### Ankle and Lower-Leg Edema

This is the most common reason women stop amlodipine. It is not cardiac edema and does not indicate heart failure. Calcium channel blockers dilate arterioles more than venules, creating a pressure gradient that shifts fluid into the ankles by late afternoon. [The FDA label](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/drugsatfda_docs/label/2011/019787s040lbl.pdf) lists edema in up to 14.6% at 10 mg but clinical practice rates are often higher in women because of the pharmacokinetic differences above.

Practical strategies: raise legs for 20 minutes after your afternoon walk, wear graduated compression stockings (15 to 20 mmHg), and avoid long periods of sitting. If edema is grade 2 or higher (pitting beyond the ankle), talk to your prescriber about adding an ARB or dropping back one dose step.

### Flushing and Headache

Flushing is most common in the first two to four weeks and usually resolves. If you are perimenopausal, log the timing of flushes: amlodipine flushing tends to occur within two to three hours of taking the tablet and lasts 15 to 30 minutes. Hot flushes from estrogen withdrawal are more random and often nocturnal. Tracking helps distinguish them.

### Palpitations and Reflex Tachycardia

Peripheral vasodilation can trigger a reflex increase in heart rate. Amlodipine's slow onset (peak plasma concentration at 6 to 12 hours) largely prevents the sharp reflex tachycardia seen with immediate-release nifedipine, but some women do notice a faster resting pulse in the first week, particularly around ovulation when baseline heart rate is naturally higher due to progesterone effects.

### Gingival Hyperplasia

Rare (&lt;1% incidence) but worth knowing: calcium channel blockers can cause gum overgrowth, especially with poor dental hygiene. Mention amlodipine to your dentist at your next cleaning.

## Amlodipine Across Your Reproductive Life

### Reproductive Years (Ages 18 to 40)

Women of reproductive age prescribed amlodipine for hypertension or PCOS-related cardiovascular risk should discuss contraception explicitly with their prescriber. Amlodipine is not a teratogen at typical doses in animals at low multiples of the human dose, but human data are limited enough that most guidelines recommend caution in the first trimester. If you are trying to conceive, have that conversation before you become pregnant, not after.

Polycystic ovary syndrome is associated with a two-to-threefold higher risk of hypertension compared with women without PCOS, [according to a 2023 meta-analysis in the Journal of Clinical Endocrinology and Metabolism](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36124376/). Women with PCOS who need antihypertensive therapy may reach for calcium channel blockers when ACE inhibitors are undesirable (e.g., in women still planning pregnancy, given ACE inhibitor teratogenicity in the second and third trimesters).

### Trying to Conceive

Calcium channel blockers in general are not associated with impaired fertility. Amlodipine does not suppress ovulation, does not affect follicle-stimulating hormone or luteinizing hormone, and has no known interaction with commonly used fertility medications such as letrozole or clomiphene. One practical note: if you are undergoing IVF, inform your reproductive endocrinologist about all antihypertensives, as blood pressure management goals shift during ovarian stimulation.

### Pregnancy

[The FDA categorized amlodipine as Pregnancy Category C](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/drugsatfda_docs/label/2011/019787s040lbl.pdf), meaning animal reproduction studies showed adverse effects and adequate, well-controlled human studies are lacking. Amlodipine is generally not a first-line antihypertensive in pregnancy. [ACOG Practice Bulletin 203 on Chronic Hypertension in Pregnancy](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/practice-bulletin/articles/2019/01/chronic-hypertension-in-pregnancy) lists labetalol, nifedipine extended-release, and methyldopa as preferred agents. Nifedipine, a close chemical relative, has substantially more human pregnancy data than amlodipine.

If you become pregnant while taking amlodipine, do not stop abruptly without speaking to your OB or MFM first. Abrupt discontinuation could cause rebound hypertension. Your care team will transition you to a safer agent.

### Postpartum and Lactation

Amlodipine is excreted in human breast milk. A [2018 case report in the Annals of Pharmacotherapy](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29081205/) measured milk-to-plasma ratios suggesting an infant would receive approximately 4 to 15% of the maternal weight-adjusted dose. This is above the threshold where most lactation experts recommend automatic avoidance, but whether it represents meaningful risk depends on infant age, weight, and health. The LactMed database, maintained by the [National Institutes of Health](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK501922/), recommends that if amlodipine is required postpartum, the infant should be monitored for hypotension, poor feeding, and drowsiness. Nifedipine has a longer safety record in lactation and is often preferred.

### Perimenopause

The overlap of perimenopausal vasomotor symptoms and amlodipine side effects is clinically significant and largely ignored in the published titration literature. {/* HRX:framework */}

A practical three-question framework for any perimenopausal woman starting amlodipine:

1. Are your flushes timed within two to three hours of tablet ingestion? If yes, suspect drug-related vasodilation rather than hormonal fluctuation.
2. Do you already have night sweats before starting amlodipine? If yes, record their frequency at baseline so you and your clinician can detect any drug-related change.
3. Are you on hormone therapy? Transdermal estradiol may slightly potentiate vasodilation; oral estrogen has a more variable effect on blood pressure. Disclose all hormone preparations to your prescriber.

Menopausal hormone therapy and amlodipine can be used together. No pharmacokinetic interaction study has compared the two directly, but the interaction is generally considered modest, and The Menopause Society's [2023 position statement on hormone therapy](https://www.menopause.org/docs/default-source/professional/2023-nams-mht-position-statement.pdf) does not list calcium channel blockers as a contraindication to hormone therapy use.

### Postmenopause

After menopause, arterial stiffness rises, isolated systolic hypertension becomes more common, and calcium channel blockers are particularly well-suited because they reduce central aortic pressure effectively. The ASCOT-BPLA data, though not sex-stratified in its primary publication, included approximately 17% women; [post-hoc analyses](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16154016/) confirmed benefit across sexes. The slow-titration approach is especially relevant here: older postmenopausal women have even slower CYP3A4 activity and lower body weight on average, meaning 2.5 mg is the appropriate starting point before any increase.

## Drug Interactions Women Should Know About

Amlodipine is a CYP3A4 substrate and a mild CYP3A4 inhibitor. Three interactions are especially relevant in women's health practice:

**Simvastatin.** Amlodipine raises simvastatin plasma levels by up to 77%. [The FDA issued a safety communication](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/drug-safety-and-availability/fda-drug-safety-communication-new-restrictions-contraindications-and-dose-limitations-zocor) recommending a simvastatin dose cap of 20 mg/day in patients taking amlodipine. This matters for postmenopausal women, for whom statin use is common.

**Cyclosporine.** Used in some autoimmune and transplant settings, cyclosporine can substantially increase amlodipine exposure. Women who take cyclosporine for lupus nephritis or after a transplant should have their blood pressure monitored more frequently after any amlodipine dose change.

**Hormonal contraceptives.** No clinically significant pharmacokinetic interaction has been demonstrated between amlodipine and combined oral contraceptives, but combined hormonal contraceptives can raise blood pressure themselves. Women who start oral contraceptives while on amlodipine should recheck blood pressure within six to eight weeks.

## Who This Titration Schedule Is Right For, and Who Should Consider an Alternative

### Good candidates for 2.5 mg slow-start titration

- Women with body weight below 60 kg (approximately 132 lbs)
- Adults over 65, regardless of sex
- Perimenopausal women with significant baseline vasomotor symptoms
- Women who previously stopped a calcium channel blocker because of flushing or edema at standard doses
- Women with liver disease, where CYP3A4 activity may be further reduced, as [the FDA label](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/drugsatfda_docs/label/2011/019787s040lbl.pdf) recommends starting at 2.5 mg in hepatic impairment
- Women on CYP3A4 inhibitors (e.g., fluconazole, diltiazem, grapefruit in large amounts)

### Women for whom amlodipine may not be the first choice

- Pregnant women, especially in the first trimester: prefer labetalol or methyldopa per [ACOG guidance](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/practice-bulletin/articles/2019/01/chronic-hypertension-in-pregnancy)
- Breastfeeding women when an alternative with better lactation data (such as nifedipine) is available
- Women with symptomatic heart failure with reduced ejection fraction: dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers are generally avoided in this setting
- Women with unstable angina or acute myocardial infarction: amlodipine is not indicated for acute management

## How to Monitor Your Response at Home

Self-monitoring with a validated upper-arm home blood pressure monitor is the standard endorsed by the [American Heart Association](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/HYPERTENSIONAHA.121.18498). Wrist monitors are less accurate and not recommended as your primary device.

Take two readings, two minutes apart, in the morning before your tablet and before coffee, and record the average. Your prescriber's office will compare these home readings to office readings to adjust your dose. For most women, the target is below 130/80 mmHg per [2017 ACC/AHA guidelines](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/HYP.0000000000000065), though individual targets vary by comorbidity.

A simple edema log: rate ankle swelling on a 0 to 3 scale each evening (0 = none, 1 = soft pitting confined to foot, 2 = pitting to mid-shin, 3 = pitting above knee or functionally limiting). Bring this log to your four-week check-in. It gives your prescriber actionable data rather than a subjective "my ankles seem puffy."

Blood pressure diaries and edema logs, when brought to appointments, measurably reduce the number of visits needed to reach a stable dose. A [2020 meta-analysis in JAMA](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2761987) found that self-monitoring combined with clinical support reduced systolic blood pressure by an additional 3.2 mmHg compared with office-only monitoring.

## Practical Tablet-Splitting and Dose Logistics

The 5 mg tablet is scored and can be split cleanly to produce 2.5 mg. The 2.5 mg tablet is also available as a branded or generic formulation in most US pharmacies and is usually priced equivalently to the 5 mg tablet, so splitting is a cost-neutral choice.

Amlodipine does not need to be taken with food, but consistency matters more than timing. Taking it every morning at breakfast is the most common approach that patients maintain long term. If you miss a dose and it has been fewer than 12 hours since your scheduled time, take it then. If more than 12 hours have passed, skip and resume the next morning. Because of the 30 to 50-hour half-life, a single missed dose has minimal impact on blood pressure control.

Store tablets below 30°C (86°F) and away from humidity. Bathroom medicine cabinets are not ideal storage because of humidity fluctuations from showers.
