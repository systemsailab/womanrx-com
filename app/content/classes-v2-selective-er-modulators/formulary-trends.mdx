# SERMs Formulary Placement Trends 2024 to 2026: What Women Need to Know

<TLDR>
Selective estrogen receptor modulators (SERMs) are shifting significantly on payer formularies between 2024 and 2026. Tamoxifen and raloxifene hold generic Tier 1 to 2 status on most plans, while newer agents like ospemifene and the enclomiphene NDA create fresh access questions for women managing breast cancer risk, osteoporosis, genitourinary syndrome, and ovulatory dysfunction.
</TLDR>

## What SERMs Are and Why They Matter Across a Woman's Life

Selective estrogen receptor modulators are small molecules that bind estrogen receptors but produce tissue-specific agonist or antagonist effects depending on the co-regulator proteins present in each tissue. That selectivity is why one molecule can protect bone, suppress breast cancer cell growth, and simultaneously avoid stimulating uterine endometrium, all depending on the tissue context and the specific drug.

For women, no other drug class spans as many clinical scenarios across the lifespan. Tamoxifen anchors adjuvant breast cancer therapy starting in the early reproductive years. Raloxifene is positioned for post-menopausal osteoporosis and breast cancer risk reduction. Ospemifene addresses genitourinary syndrome of menopause (GSM). Bazedoxifene, paired with conjugated estrogens in a single tablet, manages vasomotor symptoms without a progestogen in women with an intact uterus. Enclomiphene, the pharmacologically active trans-isomer of clomiphene, is emerging as a more targeted ovulation-induction option for women with PCOS and hypothalamic-pituitary dysfunction.

[Selective estrogen receptor modulator pharmacology is reviewed in detail in this NCBI reference](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK500922/), which underscores how co-activator versus co-repressor ratios in target tissues dictate agonist-antagonist balance.

### The Estrogen Receptor Subtype Question

Two estrogen receptor subtypes, ERα and ERβ, are distributed differently across breast, bone, uterus, brain, and cardiovascular tissues. SERMs differ in their relative affinity for each subtype. Tamoxifen is primarily an ERα antagonist in breast tissue but a partial agonist in bone and endometrium. Ospemifene shows ERβ-preferring activity in vaginal tissue, which is one mechanistic reason it does not stimulate the endometrium at standard doses. This subtype biology is directly relevant to how prescribers and payers categorize SERM risk profiles.

### Why Formulary Decisions for SERMs Are Different from Other Drug Classes

Most formulary decisions hinge on cost, efficacy, and safety. SERMs add a fourth dimension: the specific clinical indication changes the risk-benefit calculus entirely. A post-menopausal woman taking raloxifene for osteoporosis accepts a modest increase in venous thromboembolism risk in exchange for vertebral fracture reduction and breast cancer risk reduction. A pre-menopausal woman taking tamoxifen for 10 years after ER-positive breast cancer diagnosis is accepting VTE risk, hot flash burden, and endometrial hyperplasia surveillance for a survival benefit. Payers increasingly recognize that indication-specific coverage policies, not blanket Tier assignments, are the right tool for this class.

## Tamoxifen: Generic Status, Tier Placement, and What Step Edits Still Exist

Tamoxifen is off-patent and available as a cheap generic. Most commercial formularies in 2024 place it at Tier 1 or Tier 2 with no prior authorization for the two FDA-approved indications: adjuvant treatment of ER-positive breast cancer and risk reduction in high-risk women. [The FDA's current tamoxifen label](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/drugsatfda_docs/label/2021/017970s063lbl.pdf) lists the standard dose as 20 mg once daily for adjuvant therapy, and 20 mg once daily for 5 years for risk reduction in high-risk women.

Generic tamoxifen costs approximately $10, $30 per month at most retail pharmacies with GoodRx-type discount cards, making cost a minor barrier for most women who have a valid indication.

### Where Access Gaps Still Appear

Despite generic pricing, two access gaps persist in 2024.

First, extended-duration therapy (10 years rather than 5 years) remains an area where some payers require renewed prior authorization after year 5, citing the [ATLAS trial result](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22,345,399/) showing that continuing tamoxifen to 10 years reduced breast cancer recurrence risk by approximately one-third compared with stopping at 5 years. Women whose oncologists recommend extended therapy sometimes face a gap in coverage at the year-5 mark.

Second, tamoxifen for risk reduction (chemoprevention) in high-risk pre-menopausal women is under-utilized not primarily because of formulary barriers, but because uptake remains low. [A 2023 analysis in JAMA Oncology](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37439908/) found that fewer than 10% of eligible high-risk women are offered or accept chemoprevention, a gap that formulary access does not fix if the conversation never happens.

### CYP2D6 Pharmacogenomics and Coverage

Tamoxifen requires CYP2D6 metabolism to produce the active metabolite endoxifen. Women who are CYP2D6 poor metabolizers produce substantially less endoxifen and may derive less benefit from standard tamoxifen. [Pharmacogenomic data from the ABCSG-6 and ABCSG-8 trials](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19901102/) suggest that poor metabolizers on tamoxifen have worse breast cancer outcomes. Some oncologists now order CYP2D6 genotyping before selecting tamoxifen, and a handful of insurers are beginning to cover this test as part of precision oncology panels. This is an area of active coverage-policy evolution heading into 2025 to 2026.

## Raloxifene: Post-Menopausal Positioning and Tier Stability

Raloxifene 60 mg daily is generic and sits at Tier 1 to 2 on virtually all major commercial and Medicare Part D formularies in 2024. The two approved indications are post-menopausal osteoporosis treatment and prevention, and reduction of invasive breast cancer risk in post-menopausal women with osteoporosis or at high risk.

[The MORE trial](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10232912/) showed raloxifene reduced vertebral fracture risk by 30 to 50% versus placebo over 3 years in post-menopausal women with osteoporosis, and the [STAR trial](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16670411/) found raloxifene was as effective as tamoxifen in reducing invasive breast cancer risk in post-menopausal high-risk women, with fewer uterine cancers and fewer thromboembolic events than tamoxifen.

### The VTE Warning and How Payers Factor It In

Raloxifene carries a boxed warning for deep vein thrombosis and pulmonary embolism. The absolute risk increase is approximately 3 additional VTE events per 1,000 women-years of use, which is clinically meaningful for women with prior clot history. [The Menopause Society's 2023 position statement on non-hormonal management](https://www.menopause.org/docs/default-source/professional/2023-nonhormonal-ms-position-statement.pdf) notes that women with active or recent VTE should not receive raloxifene. Payers do not typically screen for VTE history at the formulary level, but step-edit requirements asking for attestation of VTE-free status are appearing in some Medicare Advantage plans.

### Raloxifene Versus Bisphosphonates in Osteoporosis

For post-menopausal osteoporosis, formulary step edits often require a trial of a bisphosphonate (typically generic alendronate) before raloxifene is covered at preferred Tier status. This is clinically reasonable for hip fracture prevention since bisphosphonates have stronger data for hip fracture reduction. Raloxifene's fracture data are primarily vertebral. Women with predominantly vertebral fracture risk on DEXA and who cannot tolerate bisphosphonates (esophageal disease, severe GI side effects) often qualify for direct Tier 2 raloxifene access with a simple letter of medical necessity.

## Ospemifene: GSM Coverage and the Branded Tier 3 Reality

Ospemifene (Osphena) is the only oral SERM approved by the FDA for moderate-to-severe dyspareunia due to vulvovaginal atrophy associated with menopause. [The FDA-approved label](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/drugsatfda_docs/label/2023/203505s010lbl.pdf) supports 60 mg once daily with food.

As of 2024, ospemifene remains branded and is placed at Tier 3 or Tier 4 on most commercial formularies, with step-edit requirements typically mandating a trial of vaginal estrogen (a generic or low-cost brand) first. This step edit is clinically contentious. Women who cannot use vaginal estrogen, including some breast cancer survivors and women with personal preferences against any hormonal therapy, may have ospemifene as their only guideline-supported oral option for GSM.

**Women most disadvantaged by the vaginal-estrogen-first step edit:**

- Post-menopausal women in geographic areas with limited gynecology access who prefer an oral agent

[ACOG Practice Bulletin 141](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/practice-bulletin/articles/2022/07/management-of-genitourinary-syndrome-of-menopause) supports ospemifene as an option for dyspareunia and does not require prior vaginal estrogen failure as a clinical prerequisite. Citing this in a prior authorization appeal is often decisive.

### What Specialty Pharmacy Requirements Mean in Practice

Some PBMs are routing ospemifene through specialty pharmacy channels despite it being an oral tablet, adding a layer of complexity. Women may find they cannot fill a standard 30-day ospemifene prescription at a retail pharmacy without a specialty pharmacy prior authorization running in parallel with the medical PA. This creates delays of 5 to 14 business days in many documented cases.

## Bazedoxifene-Conjugated Estrogens (Duavee): The Tissue-Selective Estrogen Complex

Bazedoxifene paired with conjugated estrogens (CE/BZA, branded as Duavee in the US and Duavive in Europe) is the only FDA-approved tissue-selective estrogen complex (TSEC). It is approved for moderate-to-severe vasomotor symptoms in post-menopausal women with a uterus, replacing the need for a separate progestogen. The bazedoxifene component acts as a uterine SERM antagonist, protecting the endometrium while the conjugated estrogens address hot flashes.

[The SMART (Selective estrogens, Menopause, And Response to Therapy) trial series](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19710549/) demonstrated significant reduction in hot flash frequency and severity and no increase in endometrial hyperplasia over 12 months of use.

Formulary placement in 2024 is Tier 3 on most commercial plans, with step edits requiring a prior trial of either a progestogen-containing menopausal hormone therapy or a progestogen-free option for women without a uterus. The logic is circular for the target population (women with a uterus who want to avoid progestogens), and the Menopause Society has flagged this in its formulary access advocacy documents. Prior authorization approval rates for CE/BZA improve substantially when the prescriber letter explicitly documents progestogen intolerance or contraindication.

## Enclomiphene: The Emerging Ovulation-Induction SERM

Enclomiphene is the trans-isomer of clomiphene citrate, separated from the less desirable cis-isomer (zuclomiphene). Where clomiphene citrate (Clomid, Serophene) contains a mixture of both isomers, enclomiphene delivers the hypothalamic-pituitary axis stimulatory effect more cleanly, with less anti-estrogenic activity at the endometrial and cervical mucus level, which are the two main clinical complaints associated with clomiphene use.

As of early 2025, enclomiphene does not yet have FDA approval for use in women. It has been studied primarily in men with hypogonadism, where it stimulates endogenous LH and FSH to raise testosterone. The reproductive endocrinology community has been watching closely whether an NDA for female ovulation induction will follow, particularly given interest in PCOS populations. [A published pharmacokinetic analysis in the Journal of Clinical Pharmacology](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31074498/) characterized enclomiphene's half-life as approximately 10 hours, far shorter than zuclomiphene's multi-week accumulation, which theoretically reduces the anti-estrogenic cervical effects seen with racemic clomiphene.

### PCOS and Enclomiphene: Where the Evidence Stands

Women with PCOS represent the largest population for oral ovulation-induction agents. Letrozole 2.5 to 7.5 mg on cycle days 3 to 7 is now the [ASRM-endorsed first-line agent for ovulation induction in PCOS](https://www.asrm.org/globalassets/asrm/asrm-content/news-and-publications/practice-guidelines/for-non-members/use_of_clomiphene_citrate_in_infertile_women.pdf), having outperformed clomiphene in the PPCOS II trial with a live birth rate of 27.5% versus 19.1% for clomiphene. Enclomiphene's positioning relative to letrozole in PCOS is not yet established by head-to-head data in women.

The evidence gap here is significant. Women with PCOS, who are disproportionately women of color, were not included in the enclomiphene trials conducted in men. Any extrapolation of enclomiphene's ovulation-induction profile to women with PCOS is currently hypothesis-generating, not evidence-based.

### Formulary Status of Enclomiphene in Women

Because enclomiphene does not have FDA approval for use in women as of early 2025, formulary placement is off-label. Clomiphene citrate generics remain Tier 1 to 2 and are the formulary default. Women seeking enclomiphene through compounding pharmacies face out-of-pocket costs. If an NDA for female ovulation induction is submitted and approved in the 2025 to 2026 window, expect initial Tier 3 placement with non-inferiority to letrozole required before payers consider a preferred tier.

## Pregnancy, Lactation, and Contraception: The Non-Negotiable Safety Section

This section is required reading for any woman of reproductive age considering a SERM.

### Tamoxifen

Tamoxifen is [FDA Pregnancy Category D](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/drugsatfda_docs/label/2021/017970s063lbl.pdf) and is teratogenic in animal models, with reported fetal genital tract abnormalities in women exposed during pregnancy. Any woman of reproductive potential taking tamoxifen must use effective non-hormonal contraception throughout treatment and for at least 9 months after stopping, because tamoxifen suppresses ovulation inconsistently and does not constitute reliable contraception itself. Women who wish to conceive after breast cancer treatment should discuss timing with their oncologist and reproductive endocrinologist; the POSITIVE trial ([Lancet, 2023](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736(22)02535-4/fulltext)) showed that temporary interruption of tamoxifen for planned pregnancy did not significantly worsen short-term breast cancer outcomes in a carefully selected population.

Tamoxifen is not safe during lactation. It is present in breast milk and its long-term safety for nursing infants is unknown.

### Raloxifene

Raloxifene is [contraindicated in women who are or may become pregnant](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/drugsatfda_docs/label/2018/020815s033lbl.pdf). It caused fetal skeletal and cardiovascular abnormalities in animal studies. Because raloxifene is used primarily in post-menopausal women, pregnancy is rarely a practical concern, but peri-menopausal women who still have residual ovarian function and irregular cycles must be counseled that raloxifene is not a contraceptive and that pregnancy while taking raloxifene poses fetal risk.

Raloxifene is not approved or studied in lactation.

### Ospemifene

Ospemifene is [contraindicated in pregnancy](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/drugsatfda_docs/label/2023/203505s010lbl.pdf). Because the approved indication is post-menopausal dyspareunia, pregnancy risk is low but not absent in early post-menopause when menstrual cycling has only recently stopped. Women within 12 months of their last menstrual period should have a pregnancy test before starting ospemifene.

Lactation data do not exist for ospemifene. Given the indication, it is not expected to be used during breastfeeding.

### Bazedoxifene-CE (Duavee)

CE/BZA is contraindicated in pregnancy. The conjugated estrogen component carries the same developmental risk profile as other systemic estrogens. Women who are perimenopausal and still ovulating occasionally should use contraception while taking CE/BZA. This is a commonly overlooked counseling point: vasomotor symptoms can begin years before true menopause, and a woman still having occasional periods is not yet reliably infertile.

### Enclomiphene

Women taking enclomiphene for ovulation induction should stop it as soon as a pregnancy test is positive. The drug's role is to trigger ovulation; it has no benefit and possible risk if continued after conception. Fetal safety data are sparse because the drug is new and the female indication is not yet approved. [FDA guidance on clomiphene, the parent compound](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/drugsatfda_docs/label/2012/016131s026lbl.pdf), notes no evidence of increased birth defect risk with properly timed clomiphene use, and the enclomiphene pharmacology is similar enough that this may apply, but this is extrapolation, not confirmed data.

## Who This Treatment Class Is Right For, and Who Should Look Elsewhere

### Women Most Likely to Benefit

- **Post-menopausal women with osteoporosis who also have elevated breast cancer risk**: Raloxifene is one of the only agents that simultaneously reduces vertebral fracture risk and invasive breast cancer risk, which is a meaningful co-benefit not offered by bisphosphonates.
- **ER-positive breast cancer survivors in reproductive years**: Tamoxifen remains the standard adjuvant agent for pre-menopausal women. Ten years of therapy is now the NCCN-supported standard for high-risk disease.
- **Post-menopausal women with moderate-to-severe dyspareunia who cannot or will not use vaginal estrogen**: Ospemifene at 60 mg daily is their primary oral pharmacologic option.
- **Post-menopausal women with a uterus who want estrogen for hot flashes without a progestogen**: CE/BZA is the single option in this category.
- **Women with PCOS and anovulatory infertility where letrozole has failed or is not available**: Clomiphene and potentially enclomiphene remain second-line considerations, though letrozole should be offered first per ASRM guidance.

### Women Who Should Avoid SERMs or Use with Particular Caution

- Women with active or recent DVT, PE, or stroke: all SERMs carry some thrombotic risk, with tamoxifen and raloxifene carrying boxed warnings.
- Women who are pregnant or planning pregnancy imminently: most agents are contraindicated.
- Women with unexplained uterine bleeding: tamoxifen's partial endometrial agonism can cause or mask pathology; evaluation must precede use.
- Women with severe hepatic impairment: SERM metabolism is heavily hepatic; dose adjustment or avoidance is required.

## Evidence Gaps Specific to Women: What the Trials Did Not Answer

Women have been included in SERM trials primarily when the indication was female-specific (breast cancer, osteoporosis, GSM). Enclomiphene's entire evidence base to date comes from male hypogonadism trials. PK/PD data in women across the menstrual cycle, in different hormonal milieu (natural cycle versus OCP versus post-menopausal), and in women with PCOS specifically, are essentially absent for enclomiphene.

[A systematic review in Fertility and Sterility](https://www.fertstert.org/article/S0015-0282(21)00501-1/fulltext) noted that most ovulation-induction trials have underrepresented women of color and women with BMI above 35, despite PCOS prevalence being higher in some of these groups. SERM dosing derived from predominantly White, lean trial populations may not translate cleanly to the full diversity of women seen in practice.

For tamoxifen specifically, endoxifen exposure varies up to 40-fold across CYP2D6 metabolizer phenotypes, and this variation has sex-hormone-related modifiers that remain incompletely characterized. [A 2022 paper in Clinical Pharmacology and Therapeutics](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35277979/) documented that endogenous estrogen levels modify CYP2D6 activity, meaning the same woman may convert tamoxifen differently in the pre-menopausal versus post-menopausal state. This is pharmacologically important and under-studied.

## Formulary Navigation: Practical Steps for 2024 to 2026

Getting SERM coverage in 2024 to 2026 increasingly requires proactive prescriber involvement. The most effective approach follows a predictable pattern.

First, confirm the exact formulary tier and prior authorization criteria for the specific plan year. PBM formularies can change on January 1, and a drug that was Tier 2 in December may be Tier 3 in January.

Second, document the clinical indication precisely. Generic step-edit attestations are far less effective than a letter that names the specific NCCN guideline version, the specific ACOG practice bulletin number, or the specific Menopause Society position statement, and states why the required step-therapy agent is not appropriate for this patient.

Third, use the appeals process without hesitation. Branded SERMs like ospemifene and CE/BZA have PA approval rates that significantly improve on first-level appeal when supported by guideline citations. [ACOG has published guidance on navigating insurance denials for menopause medications](https://www.acog.org/womens-health/faqs/menopause-hormone-therapy), which is a useful template.

Fourth, for women in the coverage gap period, ospemifene manufacturer copay cards (Pfizer, which markets Osphena) reduce out-of-pocket cost to approximately $0 for commercially insured women and to a fixed low copay for others, subject to eligibility. CE/BZA (Pfizer, Duavee) has a similar program. These programs do not apply to Medicare Part D plans.

If a prior authorization is denied and the appeal fails, the next step is a peer-to-peer review request. Most payers are required by state or federal law to offer a peer-to-peer call between the prescribing clinician and a payer medical director within a specified timeframe. For SERM approvals, [the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists recommends](https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/practice-bulletin/articles/2022/07/management-of-genitourinary-syndrome-of-menopause) that clinicians document patient-specific contraindications to the step-therapy requirement in writing before requesting the peer-to-peer.
